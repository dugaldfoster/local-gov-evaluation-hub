      <h2 class="section-title">What to include in a good “papers” section</h2>
      <p class="section-lede">
        Consider splitting your reading list into: (1) methodological foundations, (2) public sector evaluation standards,
        and (3) topic-specific evidence (e.g., housing, youth services, public health).
      </p>

      <div class="grid">
        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Foundations: causal inference</h3>
          <p>When you need credible claims about what caused what.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Difference-in-differences and common pitfalls</li>
            <li>Interrupted time series design and interpretation</li>
            <li>Matching methods and balance checks</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">Impact</span><span class="tag">Quasi-experimental</span></div>
        </article>

        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Foundations: implementation & complexity</h3>
          <p>When delivery matters as much as outcomes.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Implementation fidelity vs adaptation</li>
            <li>Context-mechanism-outcome thinking</li>
            <li>Learning-oriented monitoring</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">Process</span><span class="tag">Real-world</span></div>
        </article>

        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Realist and theory-based approaches</h3>
          <p>Great for programmes with multiple pathways and varied contexts.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Developing and testing programme theories</li>
            <li>Evidence mapping and “what works for whom”</li>
            <li>Using mixed methods coherently</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">Theory</span><span class="tag">Mechanisms</span></div>
        </article>

        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Economic evaluation</h3>
          <p>Useful when trade-offs matter and decision-makers need comparability.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Cost-effectiveness vs cost-benefit</li>
            <li>Valuing outcomes and avoiding over-precision</li>
            <li>Scenario and sensitivity analysis</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">VfM</span><span class="tag">Costs</span></div>
        </article>

        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Equity and distribution</h3>
          <p>Not an add-on: plan subgroup analysis and differential reach from the start.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Who is reached and retained?</li>
            <li>Do effects differ by need and disadvantage?</li>
            <li>Are there unintended harms?</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">Equity</span><span class="tag">Inclusion</span></div>
        </article>

        <article class="card" data-card>
          <h3>Reporting: what credibility looks like</h3>
          <p>Make it easier for others to judge quality.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Clear methods, limitations, and assumptions</li>
            <li>Reproducible steps (even if not full code)</li>
            <li>Accessible summaries for non-technical readers</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="meta"><span class="tag">Transparency</span><span class="tag">Communication</span></div>
        </article>
      </div>
